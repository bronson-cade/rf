    Section 3.4 Principal Ideals Domains

    Guiding Questions: Guiding Questions

    In this section, we’ll seek to answer the questions:
    🔗
    • What are principal ideal domains?
      🔗
    • What are prime and maximal ideals and how can we use them?
      🔗
    🔗
    The examples at the end of the last section were interesting. In particular, when we looked at the quotient of \(\R[x]\) by \((x^2+1)\text{.}\) In this section, we aim to give more substance to the Noether Isomorphism theorem in two ways: We isolate a class of domains where ideals are always principal and we introduce two new types of ideals. We then use these ideas to study quotients of polynomials rings.
    🔗

    Subsection 3.4.1 Principal Ideal Domains

    In \(\Z\text{,}\) and as we will see \(F[x]\) where \(F\) is a field, ideals are particularly simple in that they are always principal. We first give domains satisfying that property a name.
    🔗

    Definition 3.4.1.

    An integral domain \(R\) in which every ideal is principal is known as a principal ideal domain (PID).
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 3.4.2.

    The ring \(\Z\) is a principal ideal domain.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    The zero ideal is principally generated by \(0\) so we let \(I\subseteq\Z\) be a non-zero ideal and show it is principally generated.
    🔗
    Consider \(S=I\cap \mathbb{N}\text{.}\) Since \(I\neq \{0\}\text{,}\) there is \(0\neq a\in I\text{.}\) If \(a\ge 0\text{,}\) then \(a\in S\text{.}\) Otherwise \(-1\cdot a = -a\ge 0\) so that \(-a\in S\) (ideals are closed under multiplication). Thus \(S\) is a non-empty subset of \(\mathbb{N}\) and so it has a least element \(d\text{.}\) We claim \(I = (d)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Take any other element \(b\in I\text{,}\) which we assume is positive. Then the division algorithm tells us there exists \(q,r\in\N\) with \(0\leq r \lt d\) such that \(b = dq+r\text{.}\) But this would tell us \(r = b-dq\in I\) and \(r \lt d\text{.}\) Since \(d\) is the least positive element of \(I\text{,}\) this can only happen if \(r=0\) and so \(b=dq\) and \(b\in (d)\text{.}\) Therefore \(I=(d)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We conclude that \(\Z\) is a PID.
    🔗
    🔗
    Notice that for any non-zero ideal of \(\Z\text{,}\) the greatest common divisor of all the elements is a principal generator (the negative of it is too). We can use this to find the principal generator for ideals generated by more than one element:
    🔗

    Theorem 3.4.3.

    Let \(R\) be a principal ideal domain and \(x,y\in R\) be not both zero. Let \(I = \{xm+yn\mid m,n\in R\}\text{.}\) Then:
    1. \(I\) is an ideal, so there is some \(d\in R\) for which \(I = (d)\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(d\) is a greatest common divisor of \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    We conclude that there exist \(s,t\in R\) such that \(d = xs + yt\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Observe that \(0 = x0 + y0 \in I\text{.}\) Additionally, if \(x m_1 + y n_1, x m_2 + y n_2\in I\text{,}\) then \((x m_1 + y n_1) + (x m_2 + y n_2) = x (m_1 + m_2) + y (n_1 + n_2) \in I\text{,}\) and \(r(x m_1 + y n_1) = x (rm_1) + y(rn_1) \in I\text{.}\) Thus, \(I\) is an ideal.
    🔗
    Since \(R\) is a PID, there exists \(d\in R\) such that \(I = (d)\text{.}\) We claim that \(d\) is a GCD of \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\)
    🔗
    It is clear that \(d|x\text{,}\) as \(x\cdot 1 + y\cdot 0\in I = (d)\text{.}\) Similarly, \(d|y\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Now let \(e\in R\) be a common divisor of \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\) We wish to show that \(e|d\text{.}\) Write \(x = e k_1\) and \(y = e k_2\text{.}\) Since \(d\in (d)\text{,}\) there exist \(s,t\in R\) such that \(d = x s + yt = (e k_1) s + (e k_2)t = e(k_1 s + k_2 t)\text{,}\) and thus \(e|d\text{.}\)
    🔗
    In particular, there exist \(s,t\in R\) such that a GCD \(d\) of \(x\) and \(y\) can be written as \(d = xs + yt\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    We have so far abstracted and axiomatized several important algebraic properties of \(\Z\) that we discussed in [cross-reference to target(s) "sec_integers" missing or not unique]. In particular, we have our usual operations of addition and multiplication, and their interactions, we have notions of divisibility/factorization, irreducibility, and primality; we also have cancellation and greatest common divisors.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.4.2 Polynomial Rings over a Field

    Our next goal is to prove that polynomial rings over a field are always a PID. In the course of the proof we will notice that such rings share a lot in common (factorization-wise) with integers. The key to factorization here is the degree function.
    🔗
    We start by proving any ideal (or any non-zero subset) of a polynomial ring has an element of least degree.
    🔗

    Lemma 3.4.4.

    Let \(F\) be a field and \(S\subseteq F[x]\) a set containing a nonzero polynomial. Prove that \(S\) contains a polynomial \(f\) such that \(\deg(f) \le \deg(g)\) for all nonzero \(g\in S\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(T = \{\deg g\mid g\in S\}\text{.}\) Since \(S\) contains a nonzero polynomial, \(T\ne \emptyset\text{.}\) By WOP, \(T\) contains a minimal element \(d \ge 0\text{,}\) which must be the degree of some polynomial in \(S\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Notice that in the proof of Lemma 3.4.4 we did not use that \(F\) was a field. In particular, we only need that there are non-zero polynomials, i.e. the coefficient ring is a non-zero ring.
    🔗

    Theorem 3.4.5.

    Let \(F\) be a field. Then the ring \(F[x]\) is a principal ideal domain.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(I\) be a nonzero ideal of \(F[x]\) and let \(f(x)\in I\) be a non-zero polynomial of smallest degree. We claim \(I = (f(x))\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We have \((f(x))\subseteq I\) as \(f(x)\in I\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Let \(g(x)\in I\) and use [cross-reference to target(s) "thm_polynomialDivisionAlgorithm" missing or not unique] to write \(g(x) = f(x) q(x) + r(x)\text{,}\) where \(\deg r(x) \lt \deg f(x)\text{.}\) As in Theorem 3.4.2, write \(r(x) = g(x) - f(x) q(x) \in I\text{.}\) Since \(\deg(r(x))\lt \deg(f(x))\text{,}\) the only way this can happen is if \(r(x)=0\text{.}\) Thus, \(g(x) = f(x)q(x)\) and so \(f(x) | g(x)\text{.}\) Hence, \(g(x)\in (f(x))\text{.}\) In particular, \(I\subseteq (f(x))\) and we have \(I=(f(x))\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Thus any ideal in \(F[x]\) is principally generated and since \(F[x]\) is a domain, it is a principal ideal domain.
    🔗
    🔗
    The proof of Theorem 3.4.5 shows that to find a principal generated we need only look for a polynomial of minimal degree.
    🔗

    Example 3.4.6.

    In this example, we will show how to give a purely algebraic construction of \(\sqrt{2}\text{.}\) What does it mean to algebraically construct \(\sqrt{2}\text{?}\) We will take this to mean having access to a number system with \(\sqrt{2}\) that does not involve analytic constructions. That is, we will avoid using \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Our starting algebraic system will be the rational numbers. If we had access to analysis, we could just allow the use of \(\sqrt{2}\) by throwing it into our set. We’ve encountered this field already: \(\Q[\sqrt{2}]\text{.}\) Whatever we do should recover this field.
    🔗
    Consider the function \(\mathrm{ev}_{\sqrt{2}}\colon\mathbb{Q}[x]\to\mathbb{Q}[\sqrt{2}]\) given by \(\mathrm{ev}_{\sqrt{2}}(p(x)) = p(\sqrt{2})\text{.}\) We have seen many times that \(\mathrm{ev}_{\sqrt{2}}\) is a homomorphism. Let us find it’s kernel.
    🔗
    Since \(\mathbb{Q}[x]\) is a PID, the kernel is principally generated. Since \(\mathrm{ev}_\sqrt{2}(x^2-2) = (\sqrt{2})^2-2=0\text{,}\) the kernel is non-zero and so it is principally generated by a non-zero polynomial. By the remark before this example, it is principally generated by a polynomial of least degree. Since \(x^2-2\in\ker\mathrm{ev}_{\sqrt{2}}\text{,}\) it is a candidate for the principal generator. We just need to see no elements of smaller degree are in the kernel.
    🔗
    To that end, suppose \(a+bx\in\ker\mathrm{ev}_{\sqrt{2}}\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} 0 = \mathrm{ev}_{\sqrt{2}}(a+bx) = a+b\sqrt{2} \end{equation*}
    If \(b\neq 0\text{,}\) then \(\sqrt{2}=-a/b\) says \(\sqrt{2}\) is rational which is false. Thus \(b=0\) and so \(a=0\) too. We conclude that the kernel does not contain any terms of degrees zero or one. Hence, \(x^2-2\) is a non-zero polynomial of smallest degree in the kernel. Thus \(\ker\mathrm{ev}_{\sqrt{2}}=(x^2-2)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The evaluation mapping is onto as for any \(a+b\sqrt{2}\in\mathbb{Q}[\sqrt{2}]\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \mathrm{ev}_\sqrt{2}(a+bx) = a+b\sqrt{2}. \end{equation*}
    Thus it is an epimorphism. By the Noether Isomorphism Theorem, we have an isomorphism:
    \begin{equation*} \mathbb{Q}[x]/(x^2-2)\cong \mathbb{Q}[\sqrt{2}] \end{equation*}
    defined by \(p(x)+(x^2-2)\mapsto p(\sqrt{2})\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The ring (field more precisely) \(\mathbb{Q}[x]/(x^2-2)\) relies on:
    1. Constructing the rational numbers \(\Q\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Constructing the polynomial ring \(\Q[x]\) over \(\Q\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Modding out that polynomial ring by the principal ideal \(x^2-2\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    Each one of these steps is purely algebraic. The last step adds \(\sqrt{2}\) to \(\Q\) by saying we want to add a symbol \(x\) that squares to \(2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.4.3 Prime Ideals

    In this section, we continue return to our exploration of quotient rings by looking more closely at properties of ideals. We focus on particular properties of ideals that ensure that the quotient \(R/I\) is either a domain or a field. Our examples will focus on the case \(R\) is a PID.
    🔗

    Aside: Fraktur Font.

    The Fraktur font is a German typeface from the 16th century. It was long known as the “German typeface”. In 1941 it was banned by the Nazi government. It is usually used when denoting prime (\(\mathfrak{p}\)) and maximal (\(\mathfrak{m}\)) ideals. TBD
    🔗

    Definition 3.4.7.

    Let \(R\) be a commutative ring and \(\mathfrak{p}\subsetneq R\) a proper ideal. We say \(\mathfrak{p}\) is prime if whenever \(a,b\in R\) such that \(ab\in \mathfrak{p}\text{,}\) we have \(a\in \mathfrak{p}\) or \(b\in \mathfrak{p}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    This definition arises by replacing divides with is contained in in the usual definition of prime element??? The following theorem shows that these definitions agree to the extent they can: prime elements generate prime ideals.
    🔗

    Theorem 3.4.8.

    Let \(R\) be a domain and \(p\in R\) be prime. Then \(\mathfrak{p} = (p)\) is a prime ideal.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Suppose \(p\) is a prime element of a domain and \(\mathfrak{p} = (p)\text{.}\) Suppose a product is in \(\mathfrak{p}\text{,}\) say \(ab\in\mathfrak{p}\text{.}\) Then there exists \(r\in R\) with \(rp = ab\text{.}\) Thus \(p\mid ab\text{.}\) Since \(p\) is prime, either \(p\mid a\) or \(p\mid b\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Without loss of generality assume \(p\mid a\text{.}\) Then there exists \(s\in R\) with \(a = sp\text{.}\) Hence, \(a\in (p) = \mathfrak{p}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Activity 3.4.1.

    Which of the following ideals are prime?
    1. \((9)\) in \(\Z\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \((11)\) in \(\Z\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \((x^2+1)\) in \(\R[x]\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. \((x^2-1)\) in \(\R[x]\)
      🔗
      🔗
    5. \((x^2-5x+6, x^4+2x^3-10x^2+5x-2)\) in \(\R[x]\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. Not prime. \(3\cdot 3= 9\in (9)\text{,}\) but \(3\notin(9)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Prime. If \(x\in (11)\text{,}\) then \(11|x\) and 11 is prime.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Prime. Easy explanation is that \(R[x]/(x^2+1)\) is isomorphic to \(\C\text{.}\) \(\ddot\smile\) For now, though, we know that \(x^2+1\) is irreducible, and \(R[x]\) is a PID, so irreducibles are prime.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Not prime. \(x^2-1 = (x-1)(x+1)\text{,}\) but \(x\pm 1\notin (x^2-1)\) for degree reasons.
      🔗
      🔗
    5. One may use the EA to show that \(\gcd(x^2-5x+6,x^4+2x^3-10x^2+5x-2) = x-2\text{,}\) so \((x^2-5x+6, x^4+2x^3-10x^2+5x-2) = (x-2)\text{.}\) This is prime.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    It is this precise condition that guarantees that the resulting quotient is a domain.
    🔗

    Theorem 3.4.9.

    Let \(R\) be a commutative ring and \(I\) an ideal of \(R\text{.}\) Then \(I\) is prime if and only if \(R/I\) is an integral domain.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Begin by assuming that \(I\) is prime, and suppose \((a+I)(b+I) = 0+I = I\text{.}\) Then \(ab \in I\text{,}\) and since \(I\) is prime, either \(a\in I\) or \(b\in I\text{.}\) Thus either \(a+I = 0+I\) or \(b+I = 0+I\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Now assume that \(R/I\) is a domain. Further, let \(a,b\in R\) be such that \((a+I)(b+I) = ab+I = 0+I\text{.}\) Then \(ab\in I\text{,}\) and since \(R/I\) is a domain, \(a+I = 0+I\) or \(b+I = 0+I\text{,}\) i.e., \(a\in I\) or \(b\in I\text{.}\) Thus, \(I\) is prime.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.4.10.

    The ideal \((x^2-2)\) is a prime ideal of \(\Q[x]\) because the quotient \(\Q[x]/(x^2-2)\cong \mathbb{Q}[\sqrt{2}]\) is a field (in particular a domain).
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.4.11.

    The ideal \((x)\) is a prime ideal of \(\Z[x]\) because the quotient \(\Z[x]/(x)\cong \Z\) and \(\Z\) is a domain.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.4.12.

    The ideal \((x^2)\) is not a prime ideal of \(\Z[x]\text{.}\) Indeed, \(x\cdot x\in (x^2)\) but \(x\notin (x^2)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Alternatively, in \(\Z[x]/(x^2)\) we have \(\bar{x}\neq 0\) but \(\bar{x}\cdot \bar{x} = \bar{x}^2=0\text{.}\) Thus \(\Z[x]/(x^2)\) is not a domain and consequently \((x^2)\) is not prime.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.4.13.

    The ideal \((x,2)\) is a prime ideal of \(\Z[x]\text{.}\) Indeed, the mapping \(\varphi\colon \Z[x]\to \mathbb{F}_2\) defined by
    \begin{equation*} \varphi(a_0+a_1x+\cdots+a_nx^n) = \bar{a}_0 \end{equation*}
    is an epimorphism (exercise). This is nothing more than the reduce the constant term modulo 2 mapping.
    🔗
    The kernel of this mapping certainly contains \((x,2)\) as polynomials in \((x,2)\) are nothing more than the polynomials with even constant term, which reduces to 0 modulo 2. Any element not in \((x,2)\) has odd constant term. Thus it will not be in the kernel. Hence, \(\ker(\varphi) = (x,2)\) and by the Noether Isomorphism Theorem \(\Z[x]/(x,2)\cong\mathbb{F}_2\text{.}\) This is a field and so a domain. Thus \((x,2)\) is a prime ideal that is not principal.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.4.4 Maximal Ideals

    We now consider another important class of ideals: the maximal ideals.
    🔗

    Definition 3.4.14.

    Let \(R\) be a commutative ring and let \(\mathfrak{m}\subsetneq R\) be a proper ideal. We say that \(\mathfrak{m}\) is a maximal ideal if no proper ideal of \(R\) properly contains \(\mathfrak{m}\text{.}\) That is, if \(J\) is an ideal satisfying \(\mathfrak{m}\subseteq J\subseteq R\text{,}\) either \(J=\mathfrak{m}\) or \(J=R\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    In other words, an ideal \(\mathfrak{m}\ne R\) is maximal if no “larger” ideal (with respect to inclusion) properly contains it. As we will see later, rings can have many maximal ideals.
    🔗
    It is a fact that any ring \(R\) with \(0_R\ne 1_R\) has a maximal ideal. This follows from Zorn’s Lemma; a rigorous exploration of Zorn’s Lemma lies outside of the scope of this text, but suffice it to say that Zorn’s Lemma is incredibly useful in all areas of algebra for proving existence theorems. For example, a proof that every vector space has a basis relies on Zorn’s Lemma.
    🔗
    Rings with only one maximal ideal are said to be local rings, and are actively studied in modern research in commutative algebra (the study of commutative rings and their properties).
    🔗
    The next two results demonstrate that the maximality of \(I\) is precisely the condition that guarantees that \(R/I\) is a field.
    🔗

    Lemma 3.4.15.

    Let \(R\) be a commutative ring and \(\mathfrak{m}\) a maximal ideal of \(R\text{.}\) Let \(x\in R\setminus \mathfrak{m}\text{,}\) and set \(J = \{xr+y\mid r\in R, \ y\in \mathfrak{m}\}\text{.}\) Then: \(J\) is an ideal of \(R\) such that \(\mathfrak{m}\subsetneq J\text{.}\) Thus there exist \(r'\in R\text{,}\) \(y'\in \mathfrak{m}\) such that \(1 = xr'+y'\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(J\) be the set in question. Then \(0\in\mathfrak{m}\) as it is an ideal so \(x\cdot 0 + 0 = 0\in J\text{.}\) Take \(xr_1+y_1,xr_2+y_2\in J\text{.}\) Then:
    \begin{equation*} (xr_1+y_1)+(xr_2+y_2) = x(r_1+r_2)+(y_1+y_2) \end{equation*}
    and since \(y_1+y_2\in\mathfrak{m}\) the sum is in \(J\text{.}\) So \(J\) is closed under addition.
    🔗
    For closure by multiplication by \(R\text{:}\) Take \(r'\in R,\ xr+y\in J\) then
    \begin{equation*} r'(xr+y) = x(rr') + r'y \end{equation*}
    and since \(\mathfrak{m}\) is an ideal we have \(r'y\in\mathfrak{m}\) so that \(r'(xr+y)\in J\text{.}\) We conclude that \(J\) is an ideal.
    🔗
    For the last two statements, we know that \(\mathfrak{m}\subseteq J\text{.}\) Since \(x = x\cdot 1 + 0\in J\) and \(x\notin \mathfrak{m}\) it cannot be that they are equal so that \(\mathfrak{m}\subsetneq J\subseteq R\text{.}\) By maximality, it must be that \(J = R\text{.}\) Since \(1\in R\) there exists \(r\in R\) with \(1 = xr + y\) for some \(y\in\mathfrak{m}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 3.4.16.

    Let \(R\) be a commutative ring and \(I\) an ideal of \(R\text{.}\) Then \(I\) is maximal if and only if \(R/I\) is a field.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    If \(R/I\) is a field, assume \(J\) is an ideal of \(R\) that properly contains \(I\text{.}\) Let \(x\in J\setminus I\text{;}\) then \(x+I\) is a nonzero element of \(R/I\text{,}\) and since \(R/I\) is a field, there is some \(y+I\) such that \((xy)+I = 1+I\text{.}\) Since \(x\in J\text{,}\) \(xy\in J\text{.}\) As \(1+I = (x+I)(y+I) = xy+I\text{,}\) we have \(1-xy\in I\subsetneq J\text{,}\) and thus \(1 = (1-xy)+xy \in J\text{,}\) which means \(J = R\text{.}\) Thus, \(I\) is maximal.
    🔗
    Now, suppose that \(I\) is maximal and let \(x\in R\setminus I\text{.}\) Apply the previous lemma to obtain \(1 = xr + y'\text{,}\) where \(y\in I\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} 1 + I = xr+y + I = xr + I = (x+I)(r+I). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 3.4.17.

    Every maximal ideal is prime.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    All fields are integral domains. Thus, if \(I\) is maximal, \(R/I\) is a field, thus a domain, and thus \(I\) is prime.
    🔗
    🔗
    In general, the converse is not true (see the Challenge below). However, it holds in sufficiently nice rings.
    🔗

    Theorem 3.4.18.

    In a principal ideal domain, every prime ideal is maximal.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(R\) be a PID and \(\mathfrak{p}=(p)\) a prime ideal. By previous work, \(p\) is prime. Suppose that \(\mathfrak{p} \subseteq \mathfrak{m}\text{.}\) Thus, \(p\in (m)\text{,}\) so \(m|p\text{.}\) That is, \(p = mk\text{.}\) Since \(p\) is prime and \(R\) is a domain, it is irreducible. Thus, either \(m\) or \(k\) is a unit. If \(m\) is a unit, then \((m) = R\text{.}\) If \(k\) is a unit, then \(m = k^{-1} p\text{,}\) and thus \(m\in (p)\text{,}\) which means that \((m) = (p)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Thus, \((p)\) is maximal.
    🔗
