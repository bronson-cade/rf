Example 3.3.17. Algebraic Definition of Complex Numbers.

At this point, we’ve seen two different incarnations of the complex numbers: Definition 2.1.3 and Exercise 2.1.7.3 We will show yet another way of constructing them.
Let \(\mathrm{ev}_i\colon\R[x]\to\C\) be the evaluation at \(i\) homomorphism. Let’s also note that \(\mathrm{ev}_i(a+bx) = a+bi\text{.}\) Thus \(\mathrm{ev}_i\) is an epimorphism.
Since \(\mathrm{ev}_i(x^2+1) = i^2+1 = -1+1 = 0\) we know that \(x^2+1\in\ker\mathrm{ev}_i\text{.}\) When we get to factorizations in the next chapter we will use the division algorithm to revisit this example and prove that \(\ker\mathrm{ev}_i = (x^2+1)\text{.}\) At the moment we do not yet have the Euclidean Algorithm at our disposal. Nevertheless, it is true so by the Noether Isomorphism Theorem \(\R[x]/(x^2+1)\cong\C\text{.}\)
One could redefine the complex numbers as \(\R[x]/(x^2+1)\) and this is a purely algebraic definition of \(\C\) (up to using \(\R\)).
