Proposition 2.2.6.

Multplication of integral polynomials is commutative. That is, for any \(p(x),q(x)\in\Z[x]\text{,}\) we have \(p(x)\cdot q(x) = q(x)\cdot p(x)\text{.}\)

Proof.

Take \(p(x),q(x)\in\Z[x]\) of degrees \(m,n\text{,}\) respectively. Extend both by zero so that
\begin{align*} p(x) \amp= a_0+a_1x+\cdots +a_{m+n}x^{m+n} \\ q(x) \amp= b_0+b_1x+\cdots +b_{m+n}x^{m+n} \end{align*}
where \(a_{m+1} = \cdots = a_{m+n} = 0\) and \(b_{n+1} = \cdots = b_{m+n} = 0\text{.}\)
ðŸ”—
Then
\begin{equation*} p(x)\cdot q(x) = c_0+c_1x+\cdots + c_{m+n}x^{m+n} \end{equation*}
where
\begin{equation*} c_i = a_0b_i+a_1b_{i-1}+\cdots + a_{i-1}b_1+a_ib_0. \end{equation*}
We also have
\begin{equation*} q(x)\cdot p(x) = d_0 + d_1x + \cdots + d_{m+n}x^{m+n} \end{equation*}
where
\begin{equation*} d_i = b_0a_i + b_1a_{i-1}+\cdots + b_{i-1}a_1+b_ia_0. \end{equation*}
We need to show \(c_i=d_i\text{.}\)
ðŸ”—
To that end, recall that addition in \(\Z\) is commutative and multiplication is commutative too:
\begin{align*} c_i \amp= a_0b_i+a_1b_{i-1}+\cdots + a_{i-1}b_1+a_ib_0 \\ \amp= b_ia_0 + b_{i-1}a_1+\cdots + b_1a_{i-1}+b_0a_i \\ \amp= b_0a_i + b_1a_{i-1}+\cdots + b_{i-1}a_1+b_ia_0\\ \amp= d_i \end{align*}
Since the coefficients of \(p(x)\cdot q(x)\) and \(q(x)\cdot p(x)\) agreee they are equal polynomials.
