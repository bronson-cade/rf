Example 2.2.15. Square Matrices.

Consider the set \(M_2(\R)\) of square \(2\times 2\) matrices over \(\R\) (or whatever ring or field you like to work with). We recall matrix addition and multiplication:
\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix}a_1 \amp b_1 \\ c_1 \amp d_1 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix}a_2 \amp b_2 \\ c_2 \amp d_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}(a_1+a_2) \amp (b_1+b_2) \\ (c_1+c_2) \amp (d_1+d_2) \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
and
\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix}a_1 \amp b_1 \\ c_1 \amp d_1 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix}a_2 \amp b_2 \\ c_2 \amp d_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} a_1a_2+b_1c_2 \amp a_1b_2+b_1d_2 \\ c_1a_2+d_1c_2 \amp c_1b_2+d_1d_2 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
In Exercise???? you will check this is a ring. We show that multiplication is not commutative. To that end, consider:
\begin{equation*} A=\begin{bmatrix}1 \amp 1 \\ 1 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}, B =\begin{bmatrix}0 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
Then
\begin{equation*} A\cdot B = \begin{bmatrix}0 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}, B\cdot A = \begin{bmatrix}1 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
In particular, \(A\cdot B\ne B\cdot A\) and so matrix multiplication is not commutative.
