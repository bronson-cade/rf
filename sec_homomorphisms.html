    Section 3.1 Homomorphisms

    Guiding Questions: Guiding Questions

    In this section, we‚Äôll seek to answer the questions:
    üîó
    • What is a ring homomorphism?
      üîó
    • What are some examples of ring homomorphisms?
      üîó
    • What is the kernel and image of a homomorphism?
      üîó
    • What are the initial and terminal rings?
      üîó
    üîó
    Central to modern mathematics is the notion of function
    ‚Äâ1‚Äâ
    . This section assumes a familiarity with the idea of function from a set-theoretic point of view, as well as the concepts of injective (one-to-one), surjective (onto), and bijective functions (one-to-one correspondences).
    . Functions arise in all areas of mathematics, each subdiscipline concerned with certain types of functions. In algebra, our concern is with operation-preserving functions, such as the linear transformations \(L : V\to W\) of vector spaces you have seen in a course in linear algebra. Those linear transformations had the properties that \(L(\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{u}) = L(\mathbf{v})+L(\mathbf{u})\) (addition is preserved) and \(L(c\mathbf{u}) = c L(\mathbf{u})\) (scalar multiplication is preserved).
    üîó

    Subsection 3.1.1 Homomorphisms

    We find something similar at work in the study of homomorphisms of rings, which we define to be functions that preserve both addition and multiplication.
    üîó

    Definition 3.1.1.

    Let \(R\) and \(S\) be rings. A function \(\varphi : R\to S\) is called a ring homomorphism if it preserves addition, multiplication, and sends the identity of \(R\) to the identity of \(S\text{.}\) That is, for all \(x,y\in R\text{:}\)
    • \(\varphi(x+y) = \varphi(x) + \varphi(y)\text{,}\)
      üîó
    • \(\varphi(xy) = \varphi(x)\varphi(y)\text{,}\) and
      üîó
    • \(\varphi(1_R) = 1_S\text{.}\)
      üîó
    If \(\varphi\) is injective (one-to-one), we say that \(\varphi\) is a monomorphism. If \(\varphi\) is surjective (onto), we say that \(\varphi\) is an epimorphism. If \(\varphi\) is a bijection (both injective and surjective), we say that \(\varphi\) is an isomorphism and write \(R\cong S\text{.}\) If \(\varphi \colon R\to R\) is an isomorphism, we say \(\varphi\) is an automorphism of \(R\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó
    Our first job when glimpsing a new concept is to collect a stock of examples.
    üîó

    Exploration 3.1.1.

    Determine whether the following functions are homomorphisms, monomorphisms, epimorphisms, isomorphisms, automorphisms, or none of these. Note that \(R\) denotes an arbitrary ring and \(F\) a field.
    1. \(\varphi\colon R\to R\) defined by \(\varphi(x)=x\)
      üîó
    2. \(\psi\colon R\to R\) defined by \(\psi(x)=-x\)
      üîó
    3. \(\alpha\colon \Z\to \Z\) defined by \(\alpha(x)=5x\)
      üîó
    4. \(\mathrm{Fr}\colon \mathbb{F}_2[x]\to \mathbb{F}_2[x]\) defined by \(\mathrm{Fr}(p) = p^2\)
      üîó
    5. \(\iota\colon \mathbb{C}\to \mathbb{C}\) defined by \(\iota(a+bi)=a-bi\text{,}\) where \(a,b\in \R, i^2 = -1\)
      üîó
    6. \(\beta\colon\Z\to \Z/5\) defined by \(\beta(x) = \overline{x}\)
      üîó
    7. \(\epsilon_r\colon F[x] \to F\) defined by \(\epsilon_r(p(x)) = p(r)\text{,}\) where if \(p(x) = a_0 + a_1 x + \cdots + a_n x^n\text{,}\) \(p(r)\) is the expression obtained by ‚Äúplugging \(r\) into \(p\)‚Äù: \(p(r) = a_0 + a_1 r + \cdots + a_n r^n\) (this is known as the \(r\)-evaluation map)
      üîó
    8. \(\xi\colon \Z/5 \to \Z/10\) defined by \(\xi(\overline{x}) = \overline{5x}\)
      üîó
    üîó
    Solution.
    1. \(\varphi\colon R\to R\) defined by \(\varphi(x)=x\) is called the identity mapping. We in particular have: \(\varphi(1_R) = 1_R\text{,}\) \(\varphi(x+y) = x+y = \varphi(x)+\varphi(y)\text{,}\) and \(\varphi(xy)=xy=\varphi(x)\varphi(y).\) The identity mapping satisfies the criteria for being a ring homomorphism. It is an automorphism.
      üîó
    2. \(\psi\colon R\to R\) defined by \(\psi(x)=-x\) is the negation mapping. Although it is true that
      \begin{equation*} \psi(x+y) = -(x+y) = -x+(-y)=\psi(x)+\psi(y) \end{equation*}
      it fails the other two properties of a ring homorphism in general (sometimes not though!). Namely, for a general ring \(1_R\neq -1_R\) so that \(\psi(1_R) = -1_R\neq 1_R\) and also \(\psi(xy) = -xy\) but \(\psi(x)\psi(y) = (-x)(-y) = xy\text{.}\) In general, \(xy\neq -xy\) so \(\psi\) is not compatible with multiplication or unity. However, sometimes \(-a = a\) is true for every element of the ring, particularly Boolean rings. Examples include \(\Z/2, \Z/2[x]\text{.}\)
      üîó
    3. \(\alpha\colon \Z\to \Z\) defined by \(\alpha(x)=5x\text{,}\) or the multiplication by \(5\) map. The map \(\alpha\) does not preserve the unity nor does it preserve multiplication. Check! It is not a ring homomorphism.
      üîó
    4. \(Fr\colon \Z_2[x]\to \Z_2[x]\) defined by \(Fr(p) = p^2\) is sometimes called the Frobenius mapping. It is in fact a ring homomorphism in this case. For the unity and multiplicative compatibility we have \(Fr(1) = 1^2 = 1\) and \(Fr(pq) = (pq)^2 = p^2q^2=Fr(p)Fr(q)\text{.}\) For compatibility with addition we remind the reader that \(2\equiv_20\text{:}\)
      \begin{align*} Fr(p+q) \amp= (p+q)^2\\ \amp= p^2+2pq+q^2 \\ \amp= p^2+q^2 \\ \amp= Fr(p)+Fr(q). \end{align*}
      It is not an automorphism; however, it is a homomorphism. Homomorphisms from a ring to itself are called endomorphisms.
      üîó
    5. \(\iota\colon \mathbb{C}\to \mathbb{C}\) defined by \(\iota(a+bi)=a-bi\text{,}\) where \(a,b\in \R, i^2 = -1\) TBD
      üîó

    Historical Aside: Ferdinand Georg Frobenius.

    Ferdinand Georg Frobenius, a student of Weierstrass, (1849-1917) was a German mathematician known for contributions to number theory, group theory, differential equations, and elliptic functions.

    Example 3.1.2. The Frobenius Homomorphism.

    The Frobenius Homomorphism (or just Frobenius) is a homomorphism that exists whenever we are working with a prime. In this example, we look at the Frobenius on a polynomial ring.
    üîó
    Let \(p\) be a prime. Consider the function:
    \begin{equation*} \mathrm{Fr}\colon \mathbb{F}_p[x]\to \mathbb{F}_p[x] \end{equation*}
    defined by \(\mathrm{Fr}(f(x)) = (f(x))^p\text{.}\)
    üîó
    Before we prove that the Frobenius is a homomorphism, let‚Äôs recall that whenever \(k\neq 0,p\) the binomial coefficient \({p\choose k}\) is divisible by \(p\text{.}\) Indeed, using the usual formula:
    \begin{equation*} {p\choose k} = \frac{p!}{(p-k)!k!} = \frac{p(p-1)!}{(p-k)!k!}. \end{equation*}
    All of the terms in the denominator are strictly less than \(p\) provided \(k\ne 0,p\text{.}\) Thus there is no term in the denominator that will cancel the \(p\) in the numerator. In particular, the binomial coefficients \({p\choose k}\) are \(0\) in \(\mathbb{F}_p\) whenever \(k\ne 0,p\text{.}\)
    üîó
    With that done, let‚Äôs show \(\mathrm{Fr}\) is a homomorphism. It preserves unity: \(\mathrm{Fr}(1) = 1^p = 1\text{.}\)
    üîó
    For compatibility with addition, suppose \(f(x),g(x)\in\mathbb{F}_2[x]\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} \amp\mathrm{Fr}(f(x)+g(x)) \\ \amp= (f(x)+g(x))^p \\ \amp= f(x)^p +{p\choose 1}f(x)^{p-1}g(x) + \cdots + {p\choose p-1}f(x)g(x)^{p-1} + g(x)^p\\ \amp = f(x)^p + 0 + g(x)^p \\ \amp = \mathrm{Fr}(f(x)) + \mathrm{Fr}(g(x)) \end{align*}
    as the cross terms are zero.
    üîó
    For compatibility with multiplication:
    \begin{equation*} \mathrm{Fr}(f(x)\cdot g(x)) = (f(x)\cdot g(x))^p = f(x)^p\cdot g(x)^p = \mathrm{Fr}(f(x))\cdot \mathrm{Fr}g(x). \end{equation*}
    We conclude that the Frobenius is a homomorphism.
    üîó
    The following checks will be left as an exercise: The Frobenius is a monomorphism, it is not an epimorphism, and hence not an automorphism.
    üîó
    üîó

    Subsection 3.1.2 Properties of Homomorphisms

    The sequence of results that follows shows that homomorphisms preserve several of our ring-theoretic notions. This means Definition¬†3.1.1 is the right definition. That is, of all the possible functions between rings we isolated a good class to call structure preserving. We begin by showing that homomorphisms preserve the additive identity.
    üîó

    Theorem 3.1.3.

    Suppose \(\varphi\colon R\to S\) is a ring homomorphism. Then \(\varphi(0_R) = 0_S\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó

    Proof.

    Supppose \(\varphi\colon R\to S\) is a ring homomorphism. Note that \(0_R+0_R=0_R\) and in particular this tells us
    \begin{align*} \varphi(0_R+0_R) \amp= \varphi(0_R)\\ \varphi(0_R)+\varphi(0_R) \amp= \varphi(0_R)\\ \varphi(0_R)+\varphi(0_R) + (-\varphi(0_R))\amp= \varphi(0_R) + (-\varphi(0_R))\\ \varphi(0_R) \amp= 0_S \end{align*}
    as desired.
    üîó
    üîó
    We now show that homomorphisms preserve units.
    üîó

    Theorem 3.1.4.

    Suppose \(\varphi\colon R\to S\) is a ring homomorphism. If \(u\in R^\times \text{,}\) then \(\varphi(u)\in S^\times\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó

    Proof.

    Suppose \(\varphi\colon R\to S\) is a ring homomorphism and \(u\in R^\times \text{.}\) Then there exists \(v\in R\) with \(uv=1_R\text{.}\) We compute:
    \begin{align*} \varphi(uv) \amp= \varphi(1_R) \\ \varphi(u)\varphi(v) \amp= 1_S \end{align*}
    and hence \(\varphi(u)\in S^\times\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó
    Since homomorphisms are compatible with multiplication, and powers of elements are defined with multiplication, they must preserve powers as the next theorem shows.
    üîó

    Theorem 3.1.5.

    Suppose \(\varphi\colon R\to S\) is a ring homomorphism. For any \(a\in R\) and \(n\in\N\text{,}\) we have \(\varphi(a)^n = \varphi(a^n)\) in \(S\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó

    Proof.

    Suppose \(\varphi\colon R\to S\) is a ring homomorphism and \(a\in R\text{.}\) We now proceed by induction. The base case is \(\varphi(a) = \varphi(a)\) which certainly true.
    üîó
    For the inductive step, suppose that \(\varphi(a)^n =\varphi(a^n)\text{.}\) We show \(\varphi(a)^{n+1} = \varphi(a^{n+1})\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} \varphi(a)^{n+1}\amp= \varphi(a)\cdot \varphi(a)^n \\ \amp= \varphi(a)\cdot \varphi(a^n) \\ \amp= \varphi(a\cdot a^n) \\ \amp= \varphi(a\cdot a^{n+1}) \end{align*}
    as desired. This equality completes the proof.
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó

    Subsection 3.1.3 Kernel of a Homomorphism

    Homomorphisms give rise to a particularly important class of subsets: kernels.
    üîó

    Definition 3.1.6.

    Let \(\varphi\colon R \to S\) be a ring homomorphism. Then
    \begin{equation*} \ker \varphi =\{r\in R \mid \varphi(r)=0_S\} \end{equation*}
    is the kernel of \(\varphi\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó
    Note that by Theorem¬†3.1.3 we know \(0_R\in \ker\varphi\) always. In particular, the kernel of a ring homomorphism is always a non-empty subset.
    üîó

    Activity 3.1.2.

    For each homomorphism in Exploration¬†3.1.1, find (with justification), the kernel.
    üîó
    üîó
    Kernels are a fundamental structure when studying rings. As a first glimpse of their importance, we show that they give a useful way of determining whether their defining homomorphisms are monomorphisms.
    üîó

    Theorem 3.1.7.

    Let \(\varphi \colon R\to S\) be a homomorphism. Then \(\varphi\) is a monomorphism if and only if \(\ker\varphi = \{0\}\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó

    Proof.

    Supppose \(\varphi\) is a monomomorphism. Since \(\varphi\) is a homomorphism we know \(\varphi(0) = 0\text{.}\) Suppose now that \(x\in\ker\varphi\) so that \(\varphi(x)=0\text{.}\) Since \(\varphi\) is injective and \(\varphi(x)=\varphi(0)\text{,}\) we conclude that \(x=0\text{.}\) In particular, \(\ker\varphi=\{0\}\text{.}\)
    üîó
    For the converse, suppose that \(\ker\varphi=\{0\}\text{.}\) Suppose \(x,y\in R\) are such that \(\varphi(x) = \varphi(y)\text{.}\) Then we have:
    \begin{align*} \varphi(x)\amp= \varphi(y)\\ 0\amp=\varphi(y) -\varphi(x)\\ 0\amp=\varphi(y-x) \end{align*}
    and so \(y-x\in\ker\varphi\text{.}\) Since the only element here is \(0\text{,}\) we conclude that \(y-x=0\) or that \(y=x\text{.}\) Thus \(\varphi\) is a monomorphism.
    üîó
    üîó
    A nice application of this theorem is the following result that shows that homomorphisms from a field are always monomorphisms. They will never fail to be injective!
    üîó

    Theorem 3.1.8.

    Suppose \(\varphi\colon F\to S\) is a ring homomorphism where \(F\) is a field. Then \(\varphi\) is a monomorphism.
    üîó
    üîó

    Proof.

    Suppose \(\varphi\colon F\to S\) is a ring homomorphism where \(F\) is a field. We show \(\varphi\) is a monomorphism by applying Theorem¬†3.1.7. Suppose that there is \(x\in F\text{,}\) with \(\varphi(x) = 0\text{.}\) If \(x\neq 0\text{,}\) then there \(x\) is a unit. By Theorem¬†3.1.4, \(\varphi(x)\) is also a unit and in particular is not \(0\text{.}\) Thus the only element of \(\ker\varphi\) is \(0\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó

    Subsection 3.1.4 Image of a Homomorphism

    The image of a homomorphism \(\varphi\colon R\to S\) is just different terminology for the range. One reason to use different language is that we will see the image is not just a subset of \(S\text{,}\) it is a subring of \(S\text{!}\)
    üîó

    Definition 3.1.9.

    Suppose \(\varphi\colon R\to S\text{,}\) the image of the \(\varphi\text{,}\) denoted \(\mathrm{im}\varphi\) is the range of \(\varphi\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \mathrm{im}\varphi = \{\varphi(r)\mid r\in R\}. \end{equation*}
    üîó
    üîó

    Activity 3.1.3.

    Compute the image of the homomorphisms Exploration¬†3.1.1.
    üîó
    üîó
    Before we prove that the image of a homomorphism is a subring, let‚Äôs note that homomorphisms preserve most ring-axioms. In particular, we have defined subtraction of two ring elements \(a,b\in R\) as \(a-b:=a+(-b)\text{,}\) where \(-b\) is the additive inverse of \(b\text{.}\) In Exercise¬†3.1.7.14 you will prove that homorphisms preserve additive inverses, namely: \(\varphi(-r) =-\varphi(r)\text{.}\) In particular,
    \begin{equation*} \varphi(a)-\varphi(b) = \varphi(a)+\varphi(-b) = \varphi(a-b). \end{equation*}
    üîó

    Theorem 3.1.10.

    Suppose \(\varphi\colon R\to S\) is a homomorphism. Then \(\mathrm{im}\varphi\) is a subring of \(S\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó

    Proof.

    Since \(\varphi(1_R) = 1_S\text{,}\) we know that \(1_S\in\mathrm{im}\varphi\text{.}\) We just need to show it is closed under subtraction and multiplication:
    üîó
    For subtraction, take \(\varphi(r_1),\varphi(r_2)\in\mathrm{im}\varphi\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} \varphi(r_1)-\varphi(r_2) = \varphi(r_1-r_2)\in\mathrm{im}\varphi. \end{equation*}
    üîó
    For multiplication, we have
    \begin{equation*} \varphi(r_1)\cdot \varphi(r_2) = \varphi(r_1\cdot r_2)\in\mathrm{im}\varphi. \end{equation*}
    We conclude that \(\mathrm{im}\varphi\) is a subring of \(S\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó

    Subsection 3.1.5 The Initial Ring

    We now return to \(\Z\) and investigate homomorphisms \(\varphi\colon \Z\to R\text{,}\) where \(R\) is an arbitrary ring. We will prove that there is one and only one homomorphism. This shows that \(\Z\) is the initial ring. It is the unique ring (up to isomorphism) that has this property.
    üîó
    Before we prove this, let‚Äôs introduce some notation. Take a ring element \(a\in R\text{.}\) For \(n\in\N\) we can define
    \begin{equation*} n\cdot a = a + a + \cdots + a \end{equation*}
    where the right hand sum has \(n\) terms. Similarly,
    \begin{equation*} (-n)\cdot a = -a + (-a) + \cdots + (-a) \end{equation*}
    where the right hand sum has \(n\) terms. And \(0\cdot a=0\text{.}\) We leave it to the reader to check that this is well-defined.
    üîó

    Theorem 3.1.11.

    For any ring \(R\text{,}\) there is a unique homomorphism \(\varphi\colon \Z\to R\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó

    Proof.

    Let \(\varphi\colon \Z\to R\) be an arbitrary homomorphism. Since the image of unity is unity we have \(\varphi(1) = 1_R\text{.}\) Since the image of the additive identity is the additive identity, we have \(\varphi(0) = 0_R\text{.}\) By induction, we conclude that \(\varphi(n) = n\cdot 1_R\) for any \(n\in\N_0\text{.}\) Similarly \(\varphi(-n) = (-n)\cdot 1_R\) for any \(n\in\N\text{.}\)
    üîó
    Since \(\varphi\) was arbitrary, we have seen that any homomorphism is the homomorphism \(\varphi(a) = a\cdot 1_R\text{.}\) This proves uniqueness.
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó

    Subsection 3.1.6 The Terminal Ring

    In this last part of the section, we ask the question: Is there a ring \(S\) such that for any ring \(R\text{,}\) there exists one and only one homomorphism \(\varphi\colon R\to S\text{.}\) That is to say, is there a \(terminal ring\text{?}\) One guess might be \(\Z\text{.}\) It turns out this is not the case!
    üîó

    Activity 3.1.4.

    Verify the terminal ring, if it exists, cannot be \(\Z\text{.}\) Do so by showing there is no homomorphism \(\varphi\colon \Z/2\to \Z\text{.}\) What about \(\Z/2\text{?}\) Can it be terminal?
    üîó
    üîó
    The right choice happens to be zero!
    üîó

    Theorem 3.1.12.

    For any ring \(R\text{,}\) there is one and only one homomorphism \(\varphi\colon R\to \{0\}\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó

    Proof.

    This is an exercise. There is only one set-theoretic mapping: the zero map. In this case, we just need to see that the zero map is a homomorphism. This is the case if and only if the codomain is \(\{0\}\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó
    We now have two very special rings: \(\Z\) and \(\{0\}\text{.}\) A natural question is to ask how they interact with each other. In particular, we know there exists a unique homomorphism \(\Z\to\{0\}\text{.}\) What about the other way around?
    üîó
