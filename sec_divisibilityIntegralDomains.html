    When we introduced the notion of integral domain, we said that part of the reason for the definition was to capture some of the most essential properties of the integers. This is the heart of abstraction and generalization in mathematics: to distill the important properties of our objects of interest and explore the consequences of those properties. One such important property of \(\Z\) is cancellation.

    Subsection 2.4.1 Divisibility in Domains

    Theorem 2.4.1. Cancellation Law.

    Let \(R\) be a commutative ring. Then \(R\) is a domain if and only if for all \(a,b,c\in R\) with \(c\ne 0\) and \(ac = bc\text{,}\) we have \(a = b\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    Assume \(R\) is a domain and \(ac = bc\text{.}\) Then \(ac - bc = 0\text{,}\) so \((a-b)c = 0\text{.}\) Since \(R\) is a domain, it has no zero divisors, and therefore either \(c = 0\) or \(a-b=0\text{.}\) The first possibility cannot happen as \(c\ne 0\text{.}\) We therefore must have \(a-b = 0\text{,}\) or \(a =b\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Conversely, by contrapositive, assume that \(R\) is not a domain. Let \(z\in R\) be a zero divisor; then there is a nonzero \(x\in R\) such that \(xz = 0 = 0 z\text{.}\) Since \(z\ne 0\) as zero-divisors are non-zero, this implies that \(x = 0\) as we can cancel the \(z\)â€™s, a contradiction.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    We may read TheoremÂ 2.4.1 as saying that the defining property of an integral domain is the ability to cancel common nonzero factors. Note that we have not divided; division is not a binary operation, and nonzero elements of rings need not be units. However, as was the case in \(\Z\text{,}\) there are notions of divisibility and factorization in rings.

    Definition 2.4.2.

    Let \(R\) be a commutative ring, and let \(a,b\in R\text{.}\) We say \(a\) divides \(b\) and write \(a\mid b\) if there is a \(c\in R\) such that \(ac = b\text{.}\) We then say that \(a\) is a factor of \(b\text{.}\)

    Subsection 2.4.4 Polynomial Euclidean Algorithm

    We now specialize divisibility to polynomial rings over a field and recover many of the results we have for divisibility in \(\Z\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 2.4.9. Polynomial Division Algorithm.

    Let \(F\) be a field and \(f(x),g(x)\in F[x]\) with \(g(x)\ne 0\text{.}\) Then there exist unique \(q(x), r(x) \in F[x]\) such that
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = g(x) q(x) + r(x)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(\deg(r(x)) \lt \deg g(x)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    Existence: If \(f(x) = 0\text{,}\) then \(q(x) = 0\) and \(r(x) = 0\) will do. If \(f(x)\ne 0\) and \(\deg f \lt \deg g\text{,}\) then \(q(x) = 0\) and \(r(x) = f(x)\) will suffice. Thus, we need only consider the case in which \(f(x) \ne 0\) and \(\deg f \ge \deg g\text{.}\) We use induction on \(\deg f = m\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    When \(m = 0\text{,}\) \(\deg g \le 0 = \deg f\text{,}\) and as \(g(x) \ne 0\text{,}\) both \(f\) and \(g\) are nonzero constants. Then \(r = 0\) and \(q = fg^{-1}\) will work.
    ðŸ”—
    Now assume \(q\) and \(r\) exist whenever \(\deg f \lt m\text{.}\) Assume \(\deg f = m\) and write \(f(x) = a_0 + a_1 x + \cdots + a_m x^m\) and \(g(x) = b_0 + b_1 x + \cdots + b_n x^n\text{.}\) Use [cross-reference to target(s) "lem_polydivalglem" missing or not unique] and set \(h(x) = f(x) - a_m b_n^{-1} x^{m-n} g(x)\text{,}\) which must have degree less than \(f\text{.}\) Thus, by induction, there exist \(q_1, r_1\in F[x]\) such that \(h = g q_1 + r_1\text{,}\) with \(r_1 = 0\) or \(\deg r_1 \lt \deg g\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    We therefore have
    \begin{align*} f(x) \amp = a_m b_n^{-1} x^{m-n} g(x) + h(x)\\ \amp = a_m b_n^{-1} x^{m-n} g(x) + g(x) q_1(x) + r_1(x)\\ \amp = (a_m b_n^{-1} x^{m-n} + q_1(x)) g(x) + r_1(x)\text{,} \end{align*}
    where \(q = a_m b_n^{-1} x^{m-n} + q_1(x)\) and \(r = r_1(x)\) have the desired properties.
    ðŸ”—
    Uniqueness: Suppose \(f = gq + r\) and \(f = g\hat{q} + \hat{r}\text{,}\) where \(r,\hat{r}\) both have the desired properties. Then
    \begin{equation*} 0 = g[q - \hat{q}] + [r - \hat{r}]\text{,} \end{equation*}
    or \(\hat{r} - r = g [q - \hat{q}]\text{.}\) Thus either \(\hat{r} - r = 0\text{,}\) or \(\hat{r} - r\) has degree at least \(\deg g\text{.}\) The latter is clearly impossible, so \(\hat{r} = r\) and \(\hat{q} = q\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    The division algorithm was the crucial ingredient for the Euclidean algorithm.
    ðŸ”—

    Algorithm 2.4.10. Polynomial Euclidean Algorithm.

    Let \(F\) be a field and \(f(x),g(x)\in F[x]\) two polynomials (not both zero). To compute \(\gcd(f,g)\) construct a sequence of remainders initializing \(r_{-2} = f\) and \(r_{-1}=g\text{.}\) Set \(i=0\) then:
    1. Use the [cross-reference to target(s) "thm_polynomialDivisionAlgorithm" missing or not unique] to divide \(r_{i-2}\) by \(r_{i-1}\) with new remainder \(r_i\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} r_{i-2} = q_ir_{i-1} + r_i \end{equation*}
      where \(0\leq \deg(r_i)\lt \deg(r_{i-1})\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    2. If \(r_i(x) = 0\text{,}\) then \(\gcd(f,g) = r_{i-1}\text{.}\) STOP.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    3. Increment \(i\) by \(1\) to \(i+1\) and return to the first step.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    The Euclidean algorithm always terminates in finitely many steps as the degree of the remainders is strictly decreasing and bounded below by \(0\text{.}\) In particular, the process will terminate in at most \(\deg(f)\) steps. In each instance, we are simple dividing the previous remainder by the next to get our new remainder. We will refer to this as repeated division.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 2.4.11.

    Consider \(f(x) = x^4-1\) and \(g(x) = x^4+x\) viewed as polynomials over the field of rational numbers. Carry out repeated division:
    \begin{align*} x^4-1 \amp= 1\cdot(x^4+x)+(-x-1)\\ x^4+x \amp= (-x^3+x^2-x)(-x-1)+0 \end{align*}
    and so \(\gcd(x^4-1,x^4+x) = -x-1\text{.}\) But since greatest common divisors are only unique up to units, we say \(\gcd(x^4-1,x^4+x) = x+1\) by multiplying through by \(-1\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    The previous example shows that in polynomials over a field we can always multiply through by an appropriate constant so that the greatest common divisor is monic.

    Definition 2.4.12.

    Let \(f(x),g(x)\in F[x]\) be polynomials over a field, not both zero. The greatest common divisor is the monic polynomial which is a greatest common divisor of \(f,g\text{.}\)

    ðŸ”—

    Let \(F\) be a field. For any polynomials \(f,g\in F[x]\) not both 0, there are polynomials \(p\) and \(q\) such that
    \begin{equation*} pf + qg = \gcd(f,g)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    The Euclidean algorithm computes the greatest common divisor of \(f,g\text{.}\) After running the Euclidean Algorithm, we have a sequence of remainders and quotients \(r_i\) and \(q_i\text{,}\) respectively. Here \(r_{-2} = f\) and \(r_{-1} = g\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} r_{-2} \amp= q_0r_{-1} + r_0 \\ r_{-1} \amp = q_1r_0+r_1\\ \vdots \amp= \vdots \\ r_{n-3}\amp = q_{n-1}r_{n-2}+r_{n-1}\\ r_{n-2}\amp = q_nr_{n-1}+r_n\\ r_{n-1}\amp= q_{n+1}r_n+0 \end{align*}
    where \(r_n = \gcd(f,g)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solving this system of equations for the remainders we get:
    \begin{align*} r_{-2} -q_0r_{-1} \amp= r_0 \\ r_{-1} - q_1r_0 \amp= r_1\\ \vdots \amp= \vdots \\ r_{n-3} - q_{n-1}r_{n-2} \amp= r_{n-1}\\ r_{n-2} - q_nr_{n-1}\amp= r_n \end{align*}
    Now substitute \(r_{n-1}\) from the second to last equation into the last equation:
    \begin{align*} r_n \amp= r_{n-2}-q_nr_{n-1}\\ \amp= r_{n-2}-q_n(r_{n-3}-q_{n-1}r_{n-2}) \\ \amp= (1+q_nq_{n-1})r_{n-2}-q_nr_{n-3} \end{align*}
    and in particular we have expressed \(r_n\) as a polynomial combination of \(r_{n-2}\) and \(r_{n-3}\text{.}\)

    Into this equation substitute in \(r_{n-2}\text{,}\) which is a polynomial combination of \(r_{n-3}\) and \(r_{n-4}\text{.}\) This will express \(r_n\) as a polynomial combination of \(r_{n-3}\) and \(r_{n-4}\text{.}\) And keep doing so until we have arrived at
    \begin{equation*} \gcd(f,g) = r_n = pr_{-2}+qr_{-1} = pf+qg. \end{equation*}
    This completes the proof.
