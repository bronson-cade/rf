    Section 2.3 Integral Domains

    Guiding Questions: Guiding Questions

    In this section, we’ll seek to answer the questions:
    🔗
    • What are zero divisors?
      🔗
    • What are integral domains, and how do they relate to rings and fields?
      🔗
    🔗
    In the realm of ring theory, certain rings behave similarly to \(\Z\text{:}\) they are commutative and when we multiply any two non-zero integers it remains non-zero. Either (or both!) of these two properties are violated in general rings. We’ve already encountered commutative rings and so we begin by formalizing the latter property.
    🔗

    Subsection 2.3.1 Zero Divisors

    One of the interesting side effects of our definition of ring is that it allows for behavior that may at first appear unintuitive or downright weird.
    🔗

    Definition 2.3.1.

    A zero divisor in a ring \(R\) is a nonzero element \(z\in R\) such that there is a nonzero \(x\in R\) with \(zx = 0\) or \(xz=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Notice that the reason the idea of zero divisors at first appears weird is that they are not something we encounter when working with our familiar sets of numbers, such as \(\Z\) or \(\R\text{.}\) In fact, we specifically use the fact that there are no zero divisors in our familiar numbers systems to solve equations in high school algebra (e.g., if \((x-2)(x+5)=0\text{,}\) then \(x-2=0\) or \(x+5=0\)). The lack of zero divisors is one of the properties that does not persist in our abstraction from the integers to rings in general.
    🔗

    Exploration 2.3.1.

    Find, with justification, all of the zero divisors in \(\Z_{10}\) and \(\Z_{11}\text{.}\) Make and prove a conjecture about the existence of zero divisors in \(\Z_m\text{,}\) where \(m > 1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    The zero divisors in \(\Z_{10}\) are \(\overline{2}, \overline{4}, \overline{5}, \overline{6},\overline{8}\text{.}\) There are no zero divisors in \(\Z_{11}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    conjecture. \(\overline{x}\in \Z_m\) is a zero divisor if and only if \(\gcd(x,m) \ne 1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 2.3.2.

    Let \(R\) be a ring and suppose \(a,b\in R\) such that \(ab\) is a zero divisor. Then either \(a\) or \(b\) is a zero divisor.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(a,b\in R\) such that \(ab\) is a zero divisor. Then \(a,b\ne 0\) (else \(ab = 0\)). Since \(ab\) is a zero divisor, there is some \(c\ne 0\) such that \((ab) c = 0\text{.}\) If \(bc\ne 0\text{,}\) then \(a\) is a zero divisor, as \(a(bc) = 0\text{.}\) On the other hand, if \(bc=0\text{,}\) then \(b\) is a zero divisor, as \(b,c\ne 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    In Subsection 2.2.4 we defined units. Units are somehow opposite zero-divisors as the following theorem shows.
    🔗

    Theorem 2.3.3.

    Let \(R\) be a ring and \(u\in R^\times\text{.}\) Then \(u\) is not a zero divisor.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(u\in R^\times\) and suppose \(u\) is a zero divisor. Then there is some \(v\ne 0\) such that \(uv = 0\text{.}\) But then \(0_R = u^{-1} 0_R = u^{-1} (uv) = (u^{-1} u) v = 1_R v = v\text{.}\) \Lightning
    🔗
    🔗

    Investigation 2.3.2.

    How can we reinterpret Investigation 1.4.8 in light of our new language of units and zero divisors? State a theorem that uses this new language.
    🔗
    Answer.
    Answers may vary, but how about this: Let \(\overline{a},\overline{b},\overline{c}\in \Z_m\) such that \(\overline{a}\cdot \overline{c} = \overline{b}\cdot \overline{c}\text{.}\) Then \(\overline{a} = \overline{b}\) if \(\overline{c}\) is not a zero divisor.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.3.2 Integral Domains

    While non-commutative rings and zero divisors are of interest to mathematicians, we will focus put our focus on commutative rings without zero divisors for now. As these rings share many properties of the integers, they are known as integral domains.
    🔗

    Definition 2.3.4.

    A non-zero commutative ring \(R\) is an integral domain, or just domain, if \(R\) has no zero divisors.
    🔗
    🔗
    The next activities and theorems help us identify examples of domains, as well as situate the notion of a domain in its proper place relative to fields and rings in general.
    🔗

    Activity 2.3.3.

      🔗

    🔗

    Example 2.3.5. Gaussian Integers are a Domain.

    The Gaussian Integers \(\Z[i]\) are a domain. Indeed, they are a subring of the field \(\C\text{.}\) We will see in [cross-reference to target(s) "thm_subringOfDomainIsDomain" missing or not unique] that this is sufficient to deduce they are a domain.
    🔗
    For now, we know that as a subring of a commutative ring, the multiplication in \(\Z[i]\) is commutative. Suppose there are \(a+bi,c+di\in \Z[i]\) such that their product is zero:
    \begin{align*} 0 \amp= (a+bi)\cdot (c+di)\\ \amp=(ac-bd) + (ad+bc)i \end{align*}
    which tells us \(ac-bd=0\) and \(ad+bc=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Let’s look at \(ac=bd\text{.}\) One of two cases happend: \(a=0\) or \(a\ne 0\text{.}\) If \(a=0\text{,}\) then either \(b=0\) or \(d=0\) as \(\Z\) is a domain. If \(b=0\text{,}\) then \(a+bi=0\) as desired. If \(d=0\text{,}\) then \(ad+bc=0\) tells us \(bc=0\text{.}\) Again, \(b=0\) or \(c=0\text{.}\) If \(b=0\text{,}\) then \(a+bi=0\) and we are finished. Otherwise \(c=0\) and \(c+di=0\) and we are finished.
    🔗
    If \(a\ne 0\text{,}\) then a similar analysis shows that none of \(b,c,d\) can be zero. Multiply \(ac=bd\) through by \(d\) and multiply \(ad=-bc\) through by \(c\) we get: \(acd = bd^2\) and \(acd=-bc^2\text{.}\) Hence, \(bd^2=-bc^2\) and since \(b\ne 0\) we can cancel it to get \(d^2=-c^2\text{.}\) The left hand side is positive while the right hand side is negative, a contradiction. We conclude that \(\Z[i]\) does not have zero-divisors and it is therefore a domain.
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 2.3.6.

    Every field is a domain.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    If \(F\) is a field, the nonzero elements of \(F\) are units, which cannot be zero divisors by Theorem 2.3.3. Thus, \(F\) has no zero divisors.
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 2.3.7.

    Let \(m > 1\) and \(R = \Z_m\text{.}\) Then \(R\) is a field if and only if \(R\) is a domain.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    The forward direction holds by Theorem 2.3.6.
    🔗
    For the reverse, assume \(R\) is a domain. Then \(R\) has no zero divisors. If \(m\) is composite, there exist integers \(a,b\) satisfying \(1 \lt a,b \lt m\) such that \(m=ab\text{.}\) Then \(\overline{a},\overline{b}\ne \overline{0}\) in \(\Z_m\text{,}\) but \(\overline{a}\cdot \overline{b} = \overline{0}\text{.}\) Thus, \(m\) may not be composite, and is therefore prime. By an earlier theorem, \(\Z_p\) is a field.
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 2.3.8.

    If \(R\) is a domain and \(S\) is a subring of \(R\text{,}\) then \(S\) is a domain.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Any zero divisors in \(S\) are also zero divisors in \(R\text{.}\) Since \(R\) has no zero divisors, neither does \(S\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Note that the converse of Theorem 2.3.8 is not true. Namely, the set of \(2\times 2\)-matrices over \(\R\text{,}\) \(M_2(\mathbb{R})\text{,}\) is not a domain. Yet it contains a copy of \(\R\) as the scalar matrices \(\lambda\cdot I_2\text{.}\) Since \(\R\) is a field, it is also a domain.
    🔗

    Subsection 2.3.3 Polynomial Rings are Domains

    We now show that the property of being a domain can be passed on to polynomial rings.
    🔗

    Theorem 2.3.9.

    Suppose \(R\) is a ring. Then \(R\) is a domain if and only if \(R[x]\) is a domain.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    For the forward direction, suppose that \(R\) is a domain. Let \(f(x) = a_i x^i + a_{i+1} x^{i+1} + \cdots a_n x^n\) and \(g(x) = b_j x^j + b_{j+1} x^{j+1} + \cdots b_k x^k\) be nonzero polynomials in \(R[x]\text{,}\) where \(a_i, b_j \ne 0\text{.}\) Then the lowest-degree term in \(f(x) g(x)\) is \(a_i b_j x^{i+j}\text{.}\) Since \(R\) is a domain, \(a_i b_j\ne 0\text{,}\) and thus \(f(x)g(x)\) is not the zero polynomial. Thus \(R[x]\) has no zero-divisors. Since \(R\) is commutative, so is \(R[x]\) by Theorem 2.2.12. Thus \(R[x]\) is a domain as well.
    🔗
    For the reverse direction, suppose that \(R[x]\) is a domain. Then \(R\) can be viewed as a subring of \(R[x]\) via constant polynomials. By Theorem 2.3.8, \(R\) is a domain as well.
    🔗
    🔗

    Investigation 2.3.5.

    Is the converse of Theorem 2.3.9 true? If so, give a short proof. If not, find a counterexample.
    🔗
    Answer.
    Yes. Apply Theorem 2.3.8.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Since every field is a domain, we have the following immediate corollary of Theorem 2.3.9.
    🔗

    Corollary 2.3.10.

    Given a field \(F\text{,}\) the set of polynomials \(F[x]\) is a domain.
    🔗
    🔗
    When considering sets of polynomials, as we do in Section 1.3 (particularly in [cross-reference to target(s) "SubSec-Factoring-Polynomials" missing or not unique]), the following results will be quite useful.
    🔗

    Theorem 2.3.11.

    Let \(R\) be a domain, and let \(p(x),q(x)\in R[x]\) be nonzero polynomials. Then \(\deg(p(x)\cdot q(x)) = \deg(p(x)) + \deg(q(x))\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let the leading term of \(p(x)\) be \(a_n x^n\) and the leading term of \(q(x)\) be \(b_m x^m\text{.}\) Then the leading term of \(p(x) q(x)\) is \(a_n b_m x^{n+m}\text{.}\) (Observe that since \(R\) is a domain, \(a_n b_m \ne 0\text{.}\)) Thus, \(\deg(p(x) q(x)) = n+m = \deg(p(x)) + \deg(q(x))\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Exploration 2.3.6.

    Can the hypotheses of Theorem 2.3.11 be relaxed? If so, provide more general hypotheses and adapt the proof. If not, give an illustrative example.
    🔗
    Solution.
    If we were to relax the property of a domain we have two choices: relax commutativity or relax zero-divisors. Relaxing commutativity means we are allowing for non-commutative coefficient rings, such as \(M_2(\mathbb{Z})\text{.}\) Relaxing zero-divisors means allowing fro zero-divisors.
    🔗
    If we allow for zero-divisors, then the theorem fails: Let \(p = \overline{1} + \overline{2}x, q = \overline{2} + \overline{3}x\in \Z_6[x]\text{.}\) Observe that \(\deg(p) + \deg(q) = 1 + 1\text{,}\) but \(pq = \overline{2} + \overline{5}x\) has degree 1. This is because the leading coefficients are zero-divisors.
    🔗
    If we allow for non-commutative rings without zero-divisors (we have not encountered any of these yet), then the theorem remains true. Indeed, the leading coefficient of the product \(p(x)\cdot q(x)\) is the product of the leading coefficients. Since our ring has no zero-divisors, the same proof works!
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Investigation 2.3.7.

    Let \(R\) be a domain. What are the units of \(R[x]\text{?}\) Prove your answer.
    🔗
    Answer.
    The units are \(R^\times\text{.}\) Clearly, \(R^\times\subseteq R[x]^\times\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Suppose \(p(x),q(x)\in R[x]^\times\) such that \(p(x) q(x) = 1\text{.}\) Then \(\deg p(x) + \deg q(x) = \deg(p(x) q(x)) = \deg(1) = 0\text{.}\) Thus \(\deg p(x) = \deg q(x) = 0\text{,}\) and consequently, \(p(x),q(x)\in R^\times\text{.}\)
