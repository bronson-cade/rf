    Section 3.2 Ideals

    Guiding Questions: Guiding Questions

    In this section, weâ€™ll seek to answer the questions:
    ðŸ”—
    • What are ideals?
      ðŸ”—
    • How do ideals generalize divisibility?
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    One of the ways in which mathematicians study the structure of an abstract object is by considering how it interacts with other (related) objects. This is especially true of its subobjects. Thus, in linear algebra, we are often concerned with subspaces of a vector space as a means of understanding the vector space, or even submatrices as a way of understanding a matrix (see, e.g., the cofactor expansion formula for the determinant). In real analysis and topology, the important subobjects are usually open sets, or subsequences, and the study of a graphâ€™s subgraphs is an important approach to many questions in graph theory.
    ðŸ”—
    In this section, we begin a set-theoretic structural exploration of the notion of ring by considering a particularly important class of subsets which will be integral to our understanding of factorization.
    ðŸ”—
    These subsets are called ideals. They arose in the work of Kummer and Dedekind as a way of trying to recover some notion of unique factorization in rings that do not have properties like the fundamental theorem of arithmetic in \(\Z\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection 3.2.1 Ideals

    We first return to the kernel of a ring homomorphism introduced in SubsectionÂ 3.1.3. While the following proposition might seem odd, it will lead us to the definition of an ideal.
    ðŸ”—

    Proposition 3.2.1.

    Let \(\varphi\colon R\to S\) be a ring homomorphism and \(\ker\varphi\) be its kernel. For any \(r\in R\) and \(x,y\in \ker\varphi\text{,}\) we have:
    1. \(rx\in\ker\varphi\) and \(xr\in\ker\varphi\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    2. \(\displaystyle x+y\in\ker\varphi\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    Pick \(r\in R\) and \(x\in\ker\varphi\) so that \(\varphi(x)=0\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} \varphi(rx) = \varphi(r)\varphi(x) = \varphi(r)\cdot 0 = 0. \end{equation*}
    Thus \(rx\in \ker\varphi\text{.}\) The proof of \(xr\in\ker\varphi\) is similar and is left as an activity.
    ðŸ”—
    For the second claim, take \(x,y\in\ker\varphi\) so that \(\varphi(x)=\varphi(y)=0\text{.}\) Then we have
    \begin{equation*} \varphi(x+y) = \varphi(x)+\varphi(y) = 0 + 0 = 0. \end{equation*}
    Thus \(x+y\in\ker\varphi\text{.}\)

    The following definition of an ideal is an attempt to generalize the properties that kernels have.
    ðŸ”—

    Aside: Two-Sided Ideals.

    Our definition is that of a two-sided ideal. There are left and right ideals; however, we will not need this generality.
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 3.2.2.

    A subset \(I\) of a (not necessarily commutative) ring \(R\) is called an ideal if:
    1. \(\displaystyle 0\in I\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    2. for all \(x,y\in I\text{,}\) \(x+y\in I\text{;}\) and,
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    3. for all \(x\in I\) and for all \(r\in R\text{,}\) \(xr\in I\) and \(rx\in I\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Observe that the third requirement for a set \(I\) to be an ideal of \(R\) is simplified slightly if \(R\) is commutative. Namely, we need only check one of the conditions.
    ðŸ”—
    There are many important examples and types of ideals, but there are also some trivial ideals contained in every ring.
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 3.2.3.

    Let \(R\) be a ring. Then \(R\) and \(\{0\}\) are ideals of \(R\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    Note that \(R\subseteq R\) is a subset of \(R\) containing 0, closed under addition, and closed under multiplication. Therefore \(R\) is an ideal in \(R\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    The zero ideal \(\{0\}\) has \(0\text{.}\) The only element in \(\{0\}\) is \(0\) and \(0+0=0\in \{0\}\text{.}\) Lastly, for any \(r\in R\text{,}\) we know \(r\cdot 0 = 0\) so that \(r\cdot 0\in \{0\}\text{.}\) Thus \(\{0\}\) is an ideal of \(R\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    An ideal \(I\subseteq R\) is called proper if \(I\neq R\text{.}\) The ideals \(\{0\},R\) are called trivial ideals. If \(I\subseteq R\) is not equal to \(\{0\},R\text{,}\) it is called non-trivial.
    ðŸ”—
    The following theorem provides a useful characterization of when an ideal \(I\) is in fact the whole ring.
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 3.2.4.

    Let \(R\) be a ring and \(I\) an ideal of \(R\text{.}\) Then \(I = R\) if and only if \(I\) contains a unit of \(R\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    If \(I = R\text{,}\) \(1\in R\) will do.
    ðŸ”—
    We know that \(I\subseteq R\text{.}\) If there is a unit \(u\in I\text{,}\) then given any \(r\in R\text{,}\) \(r = r u^{-1} u = (ru^{-1}) u \in I\text{,}\) so that \(R \subseteq I\) and hence \(I=R\text{.}\)

    Subsection 3.2.2 Ideals of the Integers Modulo m

    We collect some examples of ideals. Letâ€™s first investigate the relationship between \(\Z\) and \(\Z/m\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 3.2.5.

    Pick \(m\in\mathbb{N}\) with \(m>1\text{.}\) Then the reduction modulo \(m\) map
    \begin{equation*} \varphi\colon \mathbb{Z}\to\mathbb{Z}/m \end{equation*}
    is a ring homomorphism with kernel given by
    \begin{equation*} m\mathbb{Z} = \{mk\mid k\in\mathbb{Z}\}. \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    We need to show that \(\varphi\) satisfies DefinitionÂ 3.1.1. To that end we first show it is compatible with addition. Take \(a,b\in\Z\text{.}\) Then:
    \begin{equation*} \varphi(a+b) = \overline{a+b} = \bar{a}+\bar{b} = \varphi(a)+\varphi(b). \end{equation*}
    But this is of course just the definition of addition in \(\Z/m\) which is why we spent so long checking that addition was well-defined. We were really proving this fact.
    ðŸ”—
    Similarly, for multiplication:
    \begin{equation*} \varphi(ab) = \overline{ab} = \bar{a}\bar{b} = \varphi(a)\varphi(b). \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    We also have \(\varphi(0) = \bar{0}\) and so \(\varphi\) satisfies all three propertis of being a ring homomorphism.
    ðŸ”—
    Lastly, suppose \(\varphi(a) = \bar{0}\text{.}\) That is, \(a\in\ker\vaprhi\text{.}\) Then \(\bar{a} = \bar{0}\text{.}\) But \(\bar{0} = m\Z\) and so \(\bar{a} = m\Z\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    By PropositionÂ 3.2.1 we know that \(m\Z\) is an ideal. We could have proved this directly. We do this now as it is an important skill to develop.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 3.2.6. Multiples of \(m\) in \(\Z\).

    Fix any \(m\in\Z\text{,}\) then the set of multiples of \(m\) is an ideal. That is, \(m\Z\) is an ideal. We prove this directly from the definition.
    ðŸ”—
    We have \(0 = m\cdot 0\in\Z\text{.}\) For closure under addition, take \(mk, ml\in m\Z\) for some \(k,l\in\Z\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} mk + ml = m(k+l)\in m\Z \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Since \(\Z\) is commutative, we just have to show \(m\Z\) absorbs left-multiplication. For that, take \(mk\in m\Z\) and arbitrary \(a\in\Z\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} a(mk) = m(ak)\in \Z. \end{equation*}
    Thus we have reproved TheoremÂ 3.2.5: \(m\Z\) is an ideal of \(\Z\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    If \(m=0\text{,}\) then \(0\Z = \{0\}\) is the zero ideal. If \(m=\pm 1\text{,}\) then \(m\Z = \Z\) is the other extreme. For \(|m|>1\text{,}\) the ideals \(m\Z\) are proper.

    Example 3.2.7. Matrix Rings are Simple.

    Letâ€™s consider our non-commutative example: \(M_2(\R)\text{.}\) What are the ideals here? It turns out, that this ring is simple in that it only has trivial ideals.
    ðŸ”—
    Recall, the standard basis for \(M_2(\R)\) consists of the sparse matrices \(e_{ij}\) which have a \(1\) in row \(i\) and column \(j\) and zero elsewhere. For any matrix \(A = (a_{ij})\) we have:
    \begin{equation*} e_{11}\cdot \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{12} \\ a_{21} \amp a_{22}\end{bmatrix}e_{11} = \begin{bmatrix} a_{11}\amp 0\\ 0 \amp 0\end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Similarly \(e_{12}\cdot A e_{12}\) is the matrix with \(a_{12}\) in the \((1,2)\) entry and zero elsewhere. Likewise for \(e_{21}\cdot A\cdot e_{21}\) and \(e_{22}\cdot A\cdot e_{22}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Now take any ideal \(I\subseteq M_2(\R)\text{.}\) We know that \(\{0\}, M_2(\R)\) are two ideals. What we want to show is that if \(I\neq \{0\}\text{,}\) then \(I=M_2(\R)\text{.}\) To that end, suppose \(I\neq\{0\}\) so there is a non-zero \(A\in I\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    At least one of the entries of \(A\) is non-zero, say \(a_{i,j}\neq 0\text{.}\) Then \(e_{ij}\cdot A\cdot e_{ij}\in I\) is the matrix with \(a_{ij}\) in the \((i,j)\) spot and zero elsehwere. Multiply by the scalar matrix \(\frac{1}{a_{ij}}\) so that we know \(I\) has a matrix with \(1\) in exactly one entry and zero elsewhere. That is, we know that one of the standard basis elements is in \(I\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    We now show that if one of the standard basis elements is in \(I\text{,}\) then \(I\) has all of the standard basis elements. The proof for each case is similar so we assume that \(I\) has \(e_{11} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Since \(I\) absorbs multiplication, every product we compute will be an element of \(I\text{.}\) We have:
    \begin{equation*} e_{11}\cdot e_{12} = e_{12}\in I. \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp 1 \\ 1 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}\cdot e_{12} = e_{22}\in I \end{equation*}
    and lastly \(e_{22}\cdot e_{12} = e_{12}\in I\text{.}\) Thus \(I\) has all of the elements in the standard basis.
    ðŸ”—
    To see now that \(I\) has any matrix, we take a random \(B = (b_{ij})\text{.}\) We can write
    \begin{equation*} B = b_{11}I_2\cdot e_{11} + b_{12}I\cdot e_{12} + b_{21}I\cdot e_{21} + b_{22}I\cdot e_{22} \end{equation*}
    Each one of the terms in this sum is in \(I\) since \(I\) absorbs multiplication. Since \(I\) is closed under addition, \(B\in I\text{.}\) But \(B\) was arbitrary so that \(I = M_2(\R)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    In fact, more is true! For any field \(\mathbb{F}\) and any \(n\in\mathbb{N}\text{,}\) the ring \(M_n(\mathbb{F})\) of \(n\times n\) matrices over \(\mathbb{F}\) is a simple ring. That is, it has no non-trivial ideals. The proof is similar to the case \(n=2\) and \(\mathbb{F} = \R\text{:}\) the field was irrelevant in our work and we use the matrices \(e_{ij}\) and their multiplication rules to show that we get all of \(M_n(\mathbb{R})\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Aside: Central Elements.

    In ExampleÂ 3.2.8, to show closure under multiplication by \(R\text{,}\) we used that for any polynomial \(r(x)\in R[x]\) we have \(r(x)\cdot x = x\cdot r(x)\text{.}\) If \(R\) is not commutative, then \(R[x]\) is also non-commutative. However, \(x\) is a central element of \(R[x]\text{.}\) Namely, \(x\) commutes with all polynomials.

    Example 3.2.8. Polynomials Without Constant Term.

    Letâ€™s now consider the polynomial ring \(R[x]\) over a genral ring \(R\) in the indeterminant \(x\text{.}\) Let \(I\subseteq R[x]\) be the collection of all polynomials without constant term:
    \begin{equation*} I = \{a_1x+a_2x^2+a_3x^3+\cdots + a_nx^n\mid a_1,a_2,a_3,\ldots,a_n\in R\}. \end{equation*}
    Let us check that \(I\) is an ideal. Before that, let us notice that if \(p(x)\in I\) then the lack of a constant term allows us to factor out an \(x\text{.}\) So there is some \(q(x)\) such that \(p(x) = xq(x)\text{.}\) Explicitly:
    \begin{align*} p(x) \amp=a_1x+a_2x^2+a_3x^3+\cdots + a_nx^n \\ \amp=x(a_1+a_2x+a_3x^2+\cdots + a_nx^{n-1}) \\ \amp=xq(x) \end{align*}
    where we set \(q(x) = a_1+a_2x+a_3x^2+\cdots + a_nx^{n-1}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    The zero polynomial has zero as a constant term so \(0\in I\text{.}\) Suppose we add two polynomials without constant term (possibly extend by zero so degrees match):
    \begin{align*} p(x) \amp= a_1x+\cdots + a_nx^n\\ q(x) \amp= b_1x+\cdots + b_nx^n\\ p(x)+q(x) \amp= (a_1+b_1)x+\cdots + (a_n+b_n)x^n \end{align*}
    and their sum also has no constant term. Alternatively, there exists polynomials \(p'(x),q'(x)\in R[x]\) such that \(p(x) = xp'(x)\) and \(q(x) = xq'(x)\text{.}\) Hence,
    \begin{equation*} p(x) + q(x) = xp'(x) + xq'(x) = x(p'(x)+q'(x)) \end{equation*}
    also has no constant term.
    ðŸ”—
    Suppose we have a polynomial without a constant term with a general polynomial, so \(p(x)\in I\) and \(r(x)\in R[x]\text{.}\) Then there is \(p'(x)\in R[x]\) with \(p(x) = xp'(x)\text{.}\) Hence,
    \begin{equation*} r(x)p(x) = r(x)xp'(x) = x(r(x)p'(x)) \end{equation*}
    and so the product lacks a constant term. We conclude that the polynomials without constant term collectively form an ideal \(I\) in \(R[x]\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    We now show that, at least in the commutative case, this ideal is the kernel of a familiar homomorphism. It is true in the non-commutative case as well but some care needs to be taken as the evaluation homomorphism from ExerciseÂ 3.1.7.5 is ill-behaved when \(R\) is non-commutative.
    ðŸ”—
    Suppose that \(R\) is commutative and let us consider the evaluation homomorphism
    \begin{equation*} \mathrm{ev}_{0_R}\colon R[x]\to R\text{ given by }\mathrm{ev}_0(p(x)) = p(0). \end{equation*}
    From PropositionÂ 3.2.1, the kernel of this homomorphism, \(\ker\mathrm{ev}_0\) is an ideal. We claim that it is precisely the ideal of polynomials without constant term.
    ðŸ”—
    Suppose \(\mathrm{ev}_{0_R}(p(x)) = 0\) or equivalently \(p(0_R) = 0_R\text{.}\) Writing \(p(x) = a_0+a_1x+\cdots +a_nx^n\) we have
    \begin{align*} 0_R \amp=p(0_R) \\ \amp= a_0 + a_1\cdot 0_R+\cdots + a_n\cdot 0_R^n \\ \amp= a_0 \end{align*}
    and so \(a_0=0\text{.}\) That is to say, the condition that \(p(x)\in \ker\mathrm{ev}_{0_R}\) is precisely the condition that \(p(x)\) has no constant term. Hence, \(\ker\mathrm{ev}_{0_R} = I\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    This example shows that the ideal we defined is in fact a kernel of a homomorphism. This is a general fact that will lead us to the construction of quotient rings.

    Subsection 3.2.4 Ideals Generated by a Set

    A good chunk of rings of interest are infinite and so their ideals are also usually infinite. We saw that in some cases ideals can be described by a single element, a principal generator. Rings where ideals are always generated by a single element are called principal ideal rings. These will be the subject of the next section.
