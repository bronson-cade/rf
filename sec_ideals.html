    Section 3.2 Ideals

    Guiding Questions: Guiding Questions

    In this section, we’ll seek to answer the questions:
    • What are ideals?
    • How do ideals generalize divisibility?
    One of the ways in which mathematicians study the structure of an abstract object is by considering how it interacts with other (related) objects. This is especially true of its subobjects. Thus, in linear algebra, we are often concerned with subspaces of a vector space as a means of understanding the vector space, or even submatrices as a way of understanding a matrix (see, e.g., the cofactor expansion formula for the determinant). In real analysis and topology, the important subobjects are usually open sets, or subsequences, and the study of a graph’s subgraphs is an important approach to many questions in graph theory.
    In this section, we begin a set-theoretic structural exploration of the notion of ring by considering a particularly important class of subsets which will be integral to our understanding of factorization.
    These subsets are called ideals. They arose in the work of Kummer and Dedekind as a way of trying to recover some notion of unique factorization in rings that do not have properties like the fundamental theorem of arithmetic in \(\Z\text{.}\)

    Subsection 3.2.1 Kernel Is An Ideal

    We first return to the kernel of a ring homomorphism introduced in Subsection 3.1.2. While the following proposition might seem odd, it will lead us to the definition of an ideal.

    Proposition 3.2.1.

    Let \(\varphi\colon R\to S\) be a ring homomorphism and \(\ker\varphi\) be its kernel. For any \(r\in R\) and \(x,y\in \ker\varphi\text{,}\) we have:
    1. \(rx\in\ker\varphi\) and \(xr\in\ker\varphi\)
    2. \(\displaystyle x+y\in\ker\varphi\)

    Proof.

    Pick \(r\in R\) and \(x\in\ker\varphi\) so that \(\varphi(x)=0\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} \varphi(rx) = \varphi(r)\varphi(x) = \varphi(r)\cdot 0 = 0. \end{equation*}
    Thus \(rx\in \ker\varphi\text{.}\) The proof of \(xr\in\ker\varphi\) is similar and is left as an activity.
    For the second claim, take \(x,y\in\ker\varphi\) so that \(\varphi(x)=\varphi(y)=0\text{.}\) Then we have
    \begin{equation*} \varphi(x+y) = \varphi(x)+\varphi(y) = 0 + 0 = 0. \end{equation*}
    Thus \(x+y\in\ker\varphi\text{.}\)

    Subsection 3.2.2 Ideals

    The following definition of an ideal is an attempt to generalize the properties that kernels have.

    Definition 3.2.2.

    A subset \(I\) of a (not necessarily commutative) ring \(R\) is called an ideal if:
    1. \(\displaystyle 0\in I\)
    2. for all \(x,y\in I\text{,}\) \(x+y\in I\text{;}\) and,
    3. for all \(x\in I\) and for all \(r\in R\text{,}\) \(xr\in I\) and \(rx\in I\text{.}\)
    Observe that the third requirement for a set \(I\) to be an ideal of \(R\) is simplified slightly if \(R\) is commutative. Namely, we need only check one of the conditions.
    There are many important examples and types of ideals, but there are also some trivial ideals contained in every ring.

    Theorem 3.2.3.

    Let \(R\) be a ring. Then \(R\) and \(\{0\}\) are ideals of \(R\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Note that \(R\subseteq R\) is a subset of \(R\) containing 0, closed under addition, and closed under multiplication. Therefore \(R\) is an ideal in \(R\text{.}\)
    The zero ideal \(\{0\}\) has \(0\text{.}\) The only element in \(\{0\}\) is \(0\) and \(0+0=0\in \{0\}\text{.}\) Lastly, for any \(r\in R\text{,}\) we know \(r\cdot 0 = 0\) so that \(r\cdot 0\in \{0\}\text{.}\) Thus \(\{0\}\) is an ideal of \(R\text{.}\)
    The following theorem provides a useful characterization of when an ideal \(I\) is in fact the whole ring.

    Theorem 3.2.4.

    Let \(R\) be a ring and \(I\) an ideal of \(R\text{.}\) Then \(I = R\) if and only if \(I\) contains a unit of \(R\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    If \(I = R\text{,}\) \(1\in R\) will do.
    We know that \(I\subseteq R\text{.}\) If there is a unit \(u\in I\text{,}\) then given any \(r\in R\text{,}\) \(r = r u^{-1} u = (ru^{-1}) u \in I\text{,}\) so that \(R \subseteq I\) and hence \(I=R\text{.}\)

    Subsection 3.2.3 Ideals from Reduction Modulo m

    We now illustrate how ideals and kernels are related by revisiting Section 1.4.

    Proposition 3.2.5.

    Pick \(m\in\mathbb{N}\) with \(m>1\text{.}\) Then the reduction modulo \(m\) map
    \begin{equation*} \varphi\colon \mathbb{Z}\to\mathbb{Z}/m \end{equation*}
    is a ring homomorphism with kernel given by
    \begin{equation*} m\mathbb{Z} = \{mk\mid k\in\mathbb{Z}\}. \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    We need to show that \(\varphi\) satisfies Definition 3.1.1. To that end we first show it is compatible with addition. Take \(a,b\in\Z\text{.}\) Then:
    \begin{equation*} \varphi(a+b) = \overline{a+b} = \bar{a}+\bar{b} = \varphi(a)+\varphi(b). \end{equation*}
    But this is of course just the definition of addition in \(\Z/m\) which is why we spent so long checking that addition was well-defined. We were really proving this fact.
    Similarly, for multiplication:
    \begin{equation*} \varphi(ab) = \overline{ab} = \bar{a}\bar{b} = \varphi(a)\varphi(b). \end{equation*}

    Subsection 3.2.4 Principal Ideals

    The most important type of ideals (for our work, at least), are those which are the sets of all multiples of a single element in the ring. Such ideals are called principal ideals.

    Theorem 3.2.7.

    Let \(R\) be commutative with identity and let \(a\in R\text{.}\) The set
    \begin{equation*} (a) = \{ra\mid r\in R\} \end{equation*}
    is an ideal (called the principal ideal generated by \(a\)).

    Proof.

    Observe that \(0 = 0\cdot a \in (a)\text{.}\)
    Moreover, if \(r_1 a, r_2 a\in (a)\text{,}\) \(r_1 a - r_2 a = (r_1 - r_2)a \in (a)\text{.}\) Finally, if \(x\in R\) and \(r a\in (a)\text{,}\) \(x (ra) = (xr) a \in (a)\text{.}\)
    Thus, \((a)\) is an ideal.
    The element \(a\) in the theorem is known as a generator of \((a)\text{.}\)

    Investigation 3.2.2.

    Let \(R\) be commutative with identity, and let \(x,y,z\in R\text{.}\) Give necessary and sufficient conditions for \(z\in (x)\) and, separately, \((x) \subseteq (y)\text{.}\)
    That is, fill in the blanks: “\(z\in (x) \Leftrightarrow\) _________” and “\((x)\subseteq (y) \Leftrightarrow\) _________.”
    Justify your answers.
    Answer.
    We have “\(z\in (x) \Leftrightarrow x|z\)” and “\((x)\subseteq (y) \Leftrightarrow y|x\)”

    An acceptable alternative would be: \(x\in (y)\text{.}\) Make sure students are aware of this!
    .
    Note that \(z\in (x) \Leftrightarrow \exists r\in R, z = xr \Leftrightarrow x|z\text{.}\)
    Similarly, suppose \((x)\subseteq (y)\text{.}\) Then \(x\in (y)\text{,}\) so \(y|x\text{.}\) Conversely, if \(y|x\text{,}\) then there is some \(r\in R\) such that \(x = yr\text{,}\) and thus for all \(ax\in (x)\text{,}\) \(ax = (ar)y \in (y)\text{.}\)
    Note that this means that if we want to know if \((x)\subseteq (y)\text{,}\) it’s enough to check that \(x\in (y)\text{.}\)
    Principal ideals may have more than one generator.
